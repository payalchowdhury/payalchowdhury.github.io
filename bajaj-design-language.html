Context

Why a design
language?

During the pandemic, consumer expectations were shifting fast. Bajaj, a legacy brand with over 85 years of history, initiated its "Built for Life" brand refresh — it was the perfect opportunity to champion a design-led transformation.

At the same time, our design team was growing fast, with a high volume of projects in the pipeline. Our portfolio of products was fragmented with no clear messaging — making it difficult to connect meaningfully with consumers across vastly different product categories.

The brief: create a unified visual and material language that could work across everything from a ₹500 iron to a premium kitchen appliance — coherent, scalable, and built to last.

Challenges

What made
this hard

(01)
Industry first
Creating a unified design language to support 15+ vastly differing product categories required solutions never invented before. Nothing in the industry offered a direct template — this had to be built from first principles.
(02)
High-impact programme
The work would impact every function from design to manufacturing, sales and supply chain. It meant disrupting all the processes, systems, tools and technologies that teams had grown comfortable with. Naturally, there was resistance.
(03)
Entrenched systems
Changing how a brand with 85 years of history thinks about its own product identity required sustained alignment at every level — from factory floor to boardroom. Each new principle had to be argued, tested, and proven before it could be embedded.

Implementation

Translating language
into products

Creating the design language was only half the work. Translating it into actual products — at scale, across outsourced partners, simultaneously — required a completely different kind of leadership.

I led implementation across Tata Elxsi and Capgemini Frog while coordinating with the UK agency on principle fidelity. This meant building a briefing infrastructure that non-designers could follow, a review cadence that caught deviation early, and a quality gate that held partners accountable without slowing production velocity.

The implementation programme ran in parallel across multiple product categories — each partner team working from the same design language documentation, checked against the same criteria, reviewed on the same cadence.
